This Wired World

As of December 2018, this blog is still somewhat in the works. Here I can briefly describe the project and the ideas behind it.

My Work as a Blogger

This Wired World is an effort to engage with the intersecting issues of global environmental change and technological change - in a way that extends to more everyday, relatable happenings.

The blog, while serving in part as a dumping ground for my unpublished academic writings, also contains short stories, fiction and nonfiction, and reflections on real experiences I have had.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I've always been interested in people's cultural relationships with their environments (aka Environmental Anthropology). During my year as a Masters student at the University of Chicago, I became especially preoccupied with climate change as an issue that seemed to superceding many of the traditional concerns of social scientists.

However, a problem that many peoople were pointing out was that the scientific community has consistently failed to connect issues of the global environment to everyday life. The language of climate change is often cryptic and inaccessible, and so the literature often fails to connect with ordinary people.

At the same time, a lot of scholars, especially at Uchicago, were writing on the intersection between environmental change and the history of technology. That is, not only is climate change partly a product of human technology, but our very ability to perceive, imagine, and measure climate phenomenon is predicated on specific technological capabilities. (For more on this, you should check out the work of Joe Masco and Michael Fisch, both Uchicago professors).

So, This Wired World attempts to ground these global, seemingly ephemeral issues of technology and environment in more legible wrijting - less constrained by the demands of science and academia. These issues cannot be ignored, but they must be engaged with in more than a scientific way - in a way that recognizes the mythmaking power of narrative and storytelling.

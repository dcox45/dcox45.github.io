Uchicago - Social Sciences Division

Known for its emphasis on interdisciplinary perspectives, the MAPSS (Master of Arts Program in the Social Sciences) program at the University of Chicago was a year of intense personal growth and academic output.

My work as a graduate student focused on:

  • The interplay between Technology and Environmental Change
  • The Anthropocene thesis and its critiques
  • The rise of remote sensing technology
  • New media analysis - especially Interactive Documentary
  • User Experience (UX) research into Interactive Documentary
  • Urban Geography of Canada and the United States

The Thesis Project

My thesis work examines two examples of a new form of filmmaking: the interactive documentary, also known as a web documentary. The two films, Bear71 (2012) and Fort McMoney(2013) , both use cutting edge digital storytelling techniques to explore important environmental issues in Western Canada.

Taken together, I argue that these films can provide remarkable insight into the technological and environmental conditions of our present moment - specifically in the context of the Anthropocene, the rise of remote sensing technology, and the influence of cybernetics on the thinking of artists.

To understand how these technologically complex documentaries operate, I borrow heavily from thinkers across various disciplines who theorize interactivity as a cybernetic phenomenon - that is, made possible by a real-world connectivity or intersection between humans, animals, machines, and environments.

Part of the challenge of this thesis project was getting insight into how the documentaries actually affected viewers. To help obtain this kind of data, I submitted an application for an applied research grant. The merit-based grant provides money, mentorship, and the User Experience (UX) lab & equipment of GfK in downtown Chicago.

Upon being awarded the 2016 grant, I then planned, organized, and executed a UX research study into the two interactive documentaries. This involved applying for institutional review (carried out for all research involving living human subjects), recruiting participants to watch the documentaries, and carrying out observation and interviews to obtain data.

Overall, the paper concludes that these two documentaries are exemplary of the relatively recent turn to simulation and gaming as a way of understanding, documenting, and predicting the real world (rather than using linear, fixed narratives). This turn to simulation and gaming is not limited to the world of documentary, but in fact is part of a wider reconfiguration of humanity's relationship with technology and with the natural world. For viewers, this use of simulation provides opportunities to "rehearse" one's position in a complex world, and experience a narrative on multiple sensory levels.
